Overview

Purpose

The purpose of this website is to educate college students about basic cybersecurity concepts and help them protect their personal information online. The site will provide clear explanations of common cyber threats, practical safety tips, and simple tools to help users improve their online security habits. By increasing awareness and understanding, the website aims to help users make safer decisions in their everyday digital lives.

Audience

The target audience for this website is college students, especially those with limited knowledge of cybersecurity. This includes students who regularly use the internet for school, social media, and online services but may not fully understand the risks of cyber threats. The site is designed for beginners who want simple, practical guidance on how to stay safe online.

JavaScript Usage

JavaScript will be used on the "Common Threats" page to create a dynamic, interactive experience. A dropdown menu will allow users to select a specific cybersecurity topic, and the page content will update with the relevant explanation, example, and safety tips.

Malware — Protect Your Devices

Description: Malware (malicious software) includes viruses, ransomware, and spyware designed to damage your device, steal your data, or hold your files hostage until you pay a fee.

Stats

  • New strains daily: 300k+
  • Best defense: Updates
  • Scan frequency: Weekly

Key Insights

  • Never download attachments from unknown or untrusted senders.
  • Stick to official app stores (Apple App Store, Google Play) for downloading apps.
  • Keep your operating system and browsers updated to patch security holes.
  • Use built-in security tools like Windows Defender or reputable third-party antivirus software.
  • Be cautious with free software downloads, which can sometimes bundle hidden malware.
  • Back up your important files to the cloud regularly.

Password Security — Your First Line of Defense

Description: Passwords are the keys to your digital life. Weak or reused passwords make it incredibly easy for hackers to access your email, social media, and school accounts.

Stats

  • Top hack cause: Reused PWs
  • Min length: 12 chars
  • Use a Manager: Yes

Key Insights

  • Use a unique password for every single account to prevent a domino effect if one gets breached.
  • Make passwords long (12+ characters) — length is more important than complexity.
  • Consider using a "passphrase" made of 4 random words (e.g., correct-horse-battery-staple).
  • Use a free password manager (like Bitwarden) to generate and remember all your complex passwords.
  • Never share your passwords with friends or write them down on sticky notes near your computer.
  • Turn on Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) wherever possible for an extra layer of security.

Phishing — Don't Take the Bait

Description: Phishing is when scammers send fake emails or texts pretending to be a trusted organization (like your bank or university) to trick you into revealing personal information or clicking malicious links.

Stats

  • Involved in breaches: 36%
  • Targeting students: High
  • Time to verify: 0 mins

Key Insights

  • Always check the sender's actual email address, not just the display name.
  • Beware of urgent language demanding immediate action or threatening account closure.
  • Look out for generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name.
  • Hover over links to see the real destination URL before clicking them.
  • If it looks suspicious, contact the organization directly using a known, official website.
  • Universities will rarely ask you to verify your password via an unsolicited email.

Public Wi-Fi — Browse with Caution

Description: Free Wi-Fi at coffee shops, airports, and even on campus can be convenient, but it is often unsecured. This means hackers on the same network can easily intercept the data you send and receive.

Stats

  • Risk level: High
  • Look for: HTTPS
  • Best tool: VPN

Key Insights

  • Avoid logging into sensitive accounts (like banking) while on unsecured public networks.
  • Always look for the padlock icon and "https" in the URL bar to ensure your connection to a site is encrypted.
  • Consider using a Virtual Private Network (VPN) to encrypt your traffic on public Wi-Fi.
  • Turn off file sharing and AirDrop when connected to a public network.
  • Forget public networks after you finish using them so your device does not auto-connect later.
  • If you must access something highly sensitive, use your phone's cellular hotspot instead.
